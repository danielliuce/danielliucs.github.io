Extension of Federated Learning Framework Plato
May 2022 - July

I helped extend this framework by supporting reinforcement learning training on clients. This extension allowed popular federated learning algorithms such as FedAvg and FedADP aggregate clients that used reinforcement learning.

I worked on two reinforcement learning algorithms (A2C & TD3 ) to train the clients and then used federated averaging to aggregate the clients to the server. I also helped in creating custom actor and critic models used in both A2C & TD3 as well as a custom algorithm for the server to communicate with the clients

Curriculum learning was then implemented with the aggregation algorithm federated averaging to aggregate only select clients. Experiments were ran on the adaptive bit rate environment found in park with three tasks and three seeds to test out the performance against other state of the art algorithms. Comparing curriculum learning showed better results than the classic federated learning algorithms such as FedAvg & FedADP

This work resulted in a paper submitted to the INFOCOM 2023 Conference

    • Processor Design Project
    Apr 2022

    This was a project I worked on with a good friend of mine Dante Crescenzi (insert his website here) This processor was 16 bits with 8 total registers and implemented using Verilog. I implemented assembly language instructions such as mov, movt, add, sub, ldr, str, and, b and things such as flag checking. Dante then extended the processor to support instructions such as push, pop, bl, cmp, lsl, lsr, asr, ror.

    Overall, this project taught me a lot about how a processor works and the verilog behind it. Concepts such as FSM timing diagrams were used to implement instructions on the correct clock cycle along with ModelSim debugging were extensively practiced.

    GoTime - NewHacks Hackathon Project, 4th Place Winner
    November 7th 2021

    This was a 24 hour Hackathon. It's where I first learned about Dijkstras algorithm and how it finds us the shortest possible path inside a graph. I mainly worked on the algorithm and it was then integrated with the frontend to launch a web app.The main goal of this web app was to allow University of Toronto students to have a more efficient schedule than what is currently in place.

    Currently, an example for students with three lectures will have students sometimes start at Bahen, walk up to Sidney Smith, and walk back to Bahen. Making students have two classes in Bahen and then their third and last one at Sidney Smith would be more efficient. This is what our web app aimed to do, minimize walking by rearranging schedules more efficiently. More details can be found at the projects link.

    Overall, the hackathon was lots of work as I was just at my computer for almost 24 hours but I learned a lot and it was fun.
